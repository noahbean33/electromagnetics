The Geometric Anatomy of Thermal Physics: Contact Geometry, Measure Theory, and Information Geometry in the Schuller Pedagogical Framework
The traditional exposition of thermodynamics often relies on a historical sequence of empirical observations, beginning with the efficiency of steam engines and progressing through a collection of laws labeled by integers. While pedagogically standard, this approach frequently leaves the student of theoretical physics with a sense of "conceptual clutter," where partial derivatives are manipulated without a clear underlying manifold structure. In contrast, the pedagogical philosophy of Frederic Schuller posits that physics is essentially the study of the structure of manifolds.1 This shift in perspective allows for a reformulation of thermal physics not as a set of messy empirical rules, but as a rigorous application of contact geometry, measure theory on phase space, and information geometry.3 This report examines the architecture of such a curriculum, termed "The Geometric Anatomy of Thermal Physics," which treats thermodynamic states as submanifolds and statistical ensembles as Radon-Nikodym derivatives.5

Carathéodory’s Principle as a Geometric Axiom
The Schuller approach prioritizes Carathéodory’s formulation of the Second Law over the traditional statements of Kelvin and Clausius. Carathéodory’s principle states: "In every neighborhood of any equilibrium state, there exist states that cannot be reached by adiabatic processes".10 This statement is inherently topological and leads directly to the existence of an entropy manifold.17
Using the Frobenius integrability theorem, Schuller shows that if adiabatic paths (defined by the kernel of the heat 1-form ) cannot reach all nearby states, the 1-form must possess an integrating factor.10 Geometrically, this means the state space is foliated by hypersurfaces of constant entropy.16 Temperature  emerges naturally as the inverse of the integrating factor required to make the 1-form  exact:

The Ergodic Hypothesis as Flow Transitivity
The Schuller-style curriculum reinterprets the Ergodic Hypothesis not as an assumption about time, but as a topological property of the flow on the energy hypersurface.8 Ergodicity is the condition that the Hamiltonian flow is transitive—meaning it visits every region of the energy surface with a frequency proportional to its volume.19 From a measure-theoretic standpoint, this implies that the only invariant functions on the manifold are constants (almost everywhere), allowing the replacement of time averages with integrals over the manifold volume.5

Information Geometry and the Topology of Phase Transitions

The Pedagogy of Frederic Schuller: Context and Availability
The course "Theoretische Physik 4: Statistische Physik" was taught by Frederic Schuller at the University of Erlangen-Nürnberg (FAU) as part of a rigorous four-semester sequence in theoretical physics.1 While Schuller’s English-language lectures on "The Geometric Anatomy of Theoretical Physics" and "General Relativity" have gained international acclaim on YouTube, his Statistical Physics lectures remain less accessible.1
The FAU Statistische Physik Lectures
The course delivered at FAU Erlangen (typically titled Theoretische Physik 4) followed the structure of the German "Diplom" or Master's curriculum.30 The lecture list for this series typically includes:
Foundations of measure theory and its application to phase space.
The derivation of thermodynamics from contact geometry.
Non-equilibrium statistical physics and the transition to hydrodynamics.32
These lectures are primarily available in German on platforms like FAU.tv, though student-transcribed notes in English have circulated in online physics forums.1 These notes highlight Schuller’s characteristic style: starting with axioms of set theory and manifold bundles before introducing physical concepts like pressure or temperature.2
Foundational Textbooks for the Geometric Approach
For students unable to access the Schuller lectures directly, several textbooks provide a similar manifold-centric view of thermal physics.
"The Geometry of Thermodynamic Processes" by Arjan van der Schaft: This is considered the closest modern approximation to the Schuller spirit. It treats thermodynamics through the lens of port-Hamiltonian systems and contact manifolds, emphasizing the interconnection of subsystems.34
"A Geometrical Approach to Physics" by David Burton and Adam Noble: This text provides an accessible introduction to differential forms and exterior calculus as applied to thermodynamics. It includes specific chapters on the laws of thermodynamics viewed as constraints on a differential manifold.11
"Information Geometry and Its Applications" by Shun-ichi Amari: Amari’s work is the definitive source for the Module 3 approach. He develops the Riemannian geometry of probability distributions and applies it to statistical inference and physics.22
Theoretical Synthesis and Second-Order Insights
The "Geometric Anatomy" of thermal physics reveals that the perceived complexity of thermodynamics is often an artifact of poor coordinate choices. By shifting the focus to the invariant structure of the manifold, several deeper insights emerge.
The Holonomy of Entropy
In the Carathéodory framework, the existence of entropy is a statement about the holonomy of the heat 1-form. If a system were not subject to the Second Law, the heat 1-form would have non-trivial holonomy, meaning that by following a closed loop in parameter space, one could generate an arbitrary amount of energy.15 The "foliation" into entropy surfaces is thus a geometric way of saying that the system is "well-behaved" and does not allow for perpetual motion of the second kind.10
Thermodynamic Processes as Gradient Flows
By introducing a metric on the contact manifold, non-equilibrium processes can be described as gradient flows of a thermodynamic potential.3 The Legendrian submanifold acts as a "sink" or attractor for the dynamics. This insight allows for a unified description of both reversible processes (which stay on the Legendrian manifold) and irreversible relaxations (which move toward it) using the same geometric language.3
Information Geometry as a Probe of Complexity
The divergence of the Fisher curvature at critical points suggests that phase transitions are not just changes in the state of matter, but "topological breaks" in the space of probability distributions.28 As a system approaches a phase transition, the manifold of distributions becomes extremely curved, indicating that even tiny changes in temperature or pressure lead to wildly different microstate configurations.25 This links the physics of matter to the mathematics of information in a way that is missed by traditional approaches.
Conclusion: The Future of the Geometric Approach
The Schuller-style curriculum represents the pinnacle of the "geometrization of physics" movement. By replacing heat engines with contact manifolds and sums-over-states with Radon-Nikodym derivatives, it offers a level of mathematical rigor and conceptual clarity that is often missing from undergraduate physics.2 This approach is not merely a different way of doing the same calculations; it is a fundamental shift in how we understand the nature of thermal equilibrium and the transition from the microscopic to the macroscopic world.
As fields like quantum information theory and non-equilibrium thermodynamics continue to grow, the tools of contact geometry and information geometry will become increasingly essential.5 The "Lost" Schuller lectures at FAU Erlangen, while difficult to find, have left a lasting impact on the pedagogy of theoretical physics, inspiring a new generation to view thermodynamics not as a collection of 19th-century recipes, but as the study of the beautiful, curved structure of the manifolds of nature.1
The Geometric Anatomy of Thermal Physics ensures that thermodynamics is no longer seen as an outlier in the landscape of fundamental physics. It demonstrates that the laws governing heat and entropy are as deeply rooted in geometry as General Relativity or Yang-Mills theory, providing a unified vision of the physical universe as a hierarchy of manifold structures.2 Through the lens of Schuller, thermodynamics is finally revealed for what it truly is: the elegant study of the contact geometry of our world. 2
