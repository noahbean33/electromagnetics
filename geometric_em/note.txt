The Geometrical Anatomy of Electrodynamics: A Structuralist Pedagogical Framework
The traditional instruction of electromagnetism frequently relies on heuristic models that conflate distinct mathematical objects, leading to conceptual ambiguities that persist into advanced study. This report details a rigorous pedagogical restructuring of classical electrodynamics, prioritizing the mathematical objects that carry physical concepts. The framework adheres to the principle that no physical entity should be introduced without its prerequisite mathematical structure, ensuring that every map possesses a well-defined domain, codomain, and transformation behavior.1 Central to this methodology is the rejection of the "physicist's vector"—an ill-defined object that often obscures the essential distinctions between vectors, covectors, and their various representations across affine and Euclidean spaces.1 By grounding electromagnetism in the language of differential forms, fiber bundles, and connections, this curriculum reveals the structural bifurcation of Maxwell’s equations into topological and metric-dependent components.3

Theta-Sectors and Global Geometry
A final insight is provided by the topology of the space of connections. If the gauge group  has a non-trivial fundamental group (e.g., in the case of "large" gauge transformations), the configuration space  may not be simply connected.30 This leads to the existence of different "theta-sectors" in the quantum theory—distinct vacua labeled by an angle .30 The transition between these sectors is linked to the topological charge (winding number) of the gauge transformation, a precursor to the understanding of instantons and anomalies in non-abelian theories.26
Conclusion: The Geometrical Unity of Electromagnetism
The research and pedagogical structures analyzed in this report demonstrate that electromagnetism is fundamentally a theory of geometry. By moving away from the "physicist's vector" and toward the rigorous language of manifolds and bundles, the curriculum achieves three critical goals:
Structural Clarity: It separates the topological laws () from the geometric/metric laws (), clarifying the role of gravity and spacetime curvature in electrodynamics.3
Global Understanding: It provides a natural explanation for quantization and non-local effects (Aharonov-Bohm, Dirac monopoles) through the topology of fiber bundles rather than ad-hoc semiclassical assumptions.18
Future Compatibility: It prepares the student for the Standard Model and General Relativity by using the exact mathematical tools (connections, curvature, associated bundles) that govern all fundamental forces.21
The resulting course outline provides a rigorous, 15-week progression that ensures the student never handles a physical concept without first possessing the mathematical object that carries it. This structuralist approach transforms electromagnetism from a set of empirical rules into a transparent branch of differential geometry.3
